Malindi Town: Famous Home to Portuguese's Vasco Da Gama Pillar

  • Malindi is the biggest town on the North Coast of the Kenyan Shoreline,just about 120km North of Kenya’s port city of Mombasa
  • Once a stopover to the world’s greatest explorers like Vasco da Gama, Magellan and Prince Henry the Navigator, becoming a resting point and home to many Portuguese Settlers
  • Some people say that the name is derived from a Swahili word Mali Ndi, which means a lot of wealth

Malindi is a town at the mouth of the Sabaki River, lying on the Indian Ocean coast of Kenya. It is the largest urban centre in Kilifi county

.According to Kenya National Bureau of Statistics in 2019 the town had a population of 119,859

Malindi developed as part of the emerging Swahili civilization in the 5th–10th centuries.

Malindi is known over time as a friendly ancient coastal town, founded around the 1st Century AD.

It is believed that the original site of Malindi town may have been Mambrui, a few kilometres north of the Sabaki River.

It was known to Portuguese sailors and travellers as Malinde / Melinde.

The present-day town is the second biggest town on the Kenyan Coast after Mombasa City.

Some people say that the name is derived from a Swahili word Mali Ndi, which means a lot of wealth.

However, others claim that Malindi is Swahili meaning ‘holes’ referring to the numerous caves found along the coastline.

There was a time that town was also popularly known as Malindi Mtama. This was because of the large amounts of Sorghum produced around Malindi. Mtama is Swahili for sorghum.

Vasco da gama pillar

It was built in the year 1498 by a Portuguese explorer called Vasco da Gama, hence the name of the monument. The pillar is one of the oldest European monuments in Africa

Vasco da Gama was given a warm reception from the Shiek of Malindi, compared to the hostile reception he encountered in Mombasa.

The pillar stands to this day as a fairly popular tourist attraction, though there have been calls by conservationists to take care of it since soil erosion might make the pillar fall into the ocean.

Muslim religion

The majority of the population in Malindi are Muslim having converted largely between the 13th and 14th centuries.

Export of ivory and rhino horns is Malindi’s main source of prosperity in addition to exporting agricultural products such as coconuts, oranges, millet and rice.

Italians Fell in Love with Malindi

By the 1980s and 1990s, an estimated 4,000 Italians were residents in Malindi 30,000 others visited annually.

Italians love the weather, pristine beaches, warm and welcoming locals and the abundance of seafood in Malindi.

Italians in Malindi have opened restaurants, hotels and build beach villas and his and has made the region grow economically due to the increasing number of local and international tourists visiting the area.
